# CODEX Manifesto

## Why CODEX exists

Knowledge becomes durable when it can be preserved, verified, revised, cited, and transmitted without losing the history of how it was produced.

CODEX exists to support digital scholarly editions of classical, philosophical, religious, historical, and literary texts. It provides an open architecture for separating source text, translation, commentary, metadata, editorial decisions, and publication outputs while keeping them connected through traceable structure.

CODEX does not replace philology, textual criticism, historical research, or professional publishing. It provides a reproducible framework in which these practices can work together.

## Commitments

1. Source before interpretation.
2. Translation before paraphrase.
3. Evidence before confidence.
4. Structure before presentation.
5. One master source before multiple edited copies.
6. Open formats before platform dependence.
7. Documented decisions before undocumented convention.
8. Accessibility before decorative complexity.
9. Long-term preservation before short-term convenience.
10. Human accountability for every published result.

## Architectural laws

### Knowledge must survive tools

Editors, software, file formats, publishing platforms, and AI systems may change. The intellectual record must remain transferable.

### Every important decision must be traceable

A reader or future editor should be able to determine what changed, why it changed, who approved it, and which evidence supported the decision.

### Every document must be understandable outside its original conversation

Project knowledge belongs in the repository, not only in personal memory, chat history, or undocumented workflows.

### Interpretation must never impersonate the source

Translations, reconstructions, annotations, summaries, and reflections must be clearly identified.
